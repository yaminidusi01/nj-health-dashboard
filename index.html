Hudson County: The Central Outlier

Hudson County — home to Jersey City and Hoboken — defies the statewide pattern. It carries the 4th highest diabetes burden in New Jersey while simultaneously recording the lowest depression prevalence of all 21 counties. No other county occupies this quadrant of the data. This divergence suggests that the social, demographic, and environmental factors shaping diabetes risk in Hudson County operate differently from those driving depression — a pattern worth investigating in Phase 2 with ACS socioeconomic data.

💡
Final Recommendations
Actionable implications for public health professionals, healthcare systems, and data analytics teams based on Phase 1 findings.
🔬 Research
Integrate ACS Socioeconomic Data in Phase 2
Join CDC PLACES county-level data with American Community Survey poverty rates and median household income to test whether socioeconomic factors explain diabetes and depression disparities.
🏥 Public Health
Prioritize Diabetes Prevention in Hudson County
Hudson County's diabetes burden (10.8%, 4th statewide) warrants targeted screening and prevention programs, independent of depression-focused initiatives given the conditions' distinct geographic patterns.
📊 Healthcare Analytics
Design Condition-Specific Interventions
Do not assume diabetes and depression co-occur geographically. County-level dashboards and population health tools should track each condition separately with condition-specific risk stratification.
🗺️ Resource Allocation
Use Local Data Over Statewide Averages
Statewide NJ averages (9.24% diabetes, 16.50% depression) conceal county-level variation of up to 5+ percentage points. Local-level analysis is essential for efficient public health resource allocation.
🌿 Rural NJ
Address Mental Health in Southern NJ Counties
Cape May (19.5%), Salem (19.4%), and Gloucester (19.1%) lead the state in depression despite lower diabetes burden. These counties need targeted behavioral health resources and access improvements.
🔍 Limitations
Acknowledge Ecological Analysis Constraints
County-level findings reflect area averages, not individual risk. Correlation does not imply causation. ACS integration and multilevel modeling in Phase 2 will strengthen causal inference.
